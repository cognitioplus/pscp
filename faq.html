DDRiVE-M PSCP FAQ

Understanding the Public Service Continuity Plan Framework

Frequently Asked Questions

I. General Overview

The Data-Driven Risk and Vulnerability Evaluation and Management (DDRiVE-M) Platform is a tool and framework designed to promote **Public Service Continuity (PSCP)** for government agencies. It facilitates the planning, analysis, and activation of protocols to ensure essential services continue to be delivered during and after a disruptive incident.

The platform focuses on **Public Service Continuity (PSCP)**, which is the organization's ability to perform its legal mandate when a disaster strikes. This differs from standard emergency response, which typically focuses on saving lives and property.

The DDRiVE-M is compliant with both **ISO (International Organization for Standardization)** and **Philippine Frameworks** in Risk Management.

II. Planning and Analysis

The process is broken down into three main phases:

  • Policy & Objective: Establishing the continuity policy, scope, and objectives.
  • Risk & Impact Analysis: Identifying essential functions and assessing potential threats through Risk Assessment and Business Impact Analysis (BIA).
  • Response & Recovery: Developing strategies and procedures to restore services within the defined Recovery Time Objective (RTO).

RTO (Recovery Time Objective): This is the maximum time a critical service can be down following a disruption before the agency experiences unacceptable consequences. Shorter RTOs indicate higher urgency.

RPO (Recovery Point Objective): This defines how much data loss is acceptable, typically measured in time (e.g., 1 hour of data loss).

The Risk Assessment Matrix helps visualize the **Likelihood vs. Impact** of potential hazards. Any hazard plotted in the **High Impact + High Likelihood (Top Right)** quadrant requires immediate mitigation strategies. [Image of Risk Assessment Matrix]

III. Continuity Strategies

Alternate sites are designated locations to relocate critical staff and operations if the primary facility is unavailable. They include:

  • Hot Site: Fully equipped and ready for immediate use.
  • Warm Site: Partially equipped, requiring some preparation time.
  • Cold Site: Space only, requiring significant time and resources to become operational.

The 3-2-1 Rule is a best practice for data backup and protection of essential documents, stating you should have:

  • 3 Copies of your data.
  • 2 Different media types (e.g., hard drive and cloud storage).
  • 1 Off-site location (a separate geographical location).

IV. Governance and Activation

The **Continuity Management Team (CMT)** is the decision-making body during a crisis. Key roles within the CMT typically include:

  • CMT Leader (Head of Agency)
  • Operations Lead (Service Recovery)
  • Logistics Lead (Resources & Sites)
  • Comms & IT Lead (Information & Technology)

The Activation Protocol details the procedures for the initial hours of a disruptive incident. The key steps are:

  1. **Incident Detection:** Security or staff reports an anomaly.
  2. **Initial Assessment:** The CMT determines the impact level.
  3. **Activation Decision:** The Head of Agency formally activates the PSCP.
  4. **Notification:** Call trees and communication plans are initiated.
  5. **Relocation:** Critical personnel move to the Alternate Site, if necessary.
